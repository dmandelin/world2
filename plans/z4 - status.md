Status is a ground for conflict, so we need to understand it
better. Relative to our needs, introductory materials are
pretty murky, emphasizing the "insulting the meat" egalitarianism,
but the descriptions make those societies actually sound like
hotbeds of rivalry that is kept within certain bounds. We need
to understand the real dynamics and change possibilities, not
just commonly observed metastable states.

One key concept is that status may not actually be a real thing.
People may want both the best doctors and the best comedians, but
those would be two different kinds of status. There may end up
being some meaningful summary stats on this, but we don't necessarily
need to posit that there is one upfront. And in sub-Dunbar's-number
groups, people may be able to largely sync on judgments. Another
idea to keep in mind is that if the summary values are generally
synthetic, perhaps we should report variances and distributions as
well, to highlight the diversity of opinion.

The biggest missing pieces in our model are:

*   What is status, or our relevant status-like concepts?
*   What evolutionary effects does status have?
*   What other key life-history effects does status have?

I'll take the first question last, attempting to synthesize the
other answers.

## Primary evolutionary effects of status

This is somewhat different for men and women, and marriage norms
also matter:

*   Women
    *   Attract quality spouse
    *   Help acquiring resources (? find out more how this would work)
    *   Alloparenting partners
*   Men in polygynous societies
    *   Attract more spouses
    *   Attract higher-quality spouses (but less emphasized)
    *   Marry earlier
*   Men in monogamous societies
    *   Attract higher-quality spouses
    *   Marry earlier

## Other effects of status

*   Lower psychosocial stress/cortisol - but can be nuanced
*   Access to
    *   Capital
    *   Mentors/Information
    *   Labor
*   Influence over group decisions
    *   In particular via others wanting to agree

## What is status?

The more fundamental concepts seem to be preference, deference,
and memberhip judgments, all of which are potentially personal
and situational. However, exchange can synchronize things:

*   When clans exchange information about judgments and judgment
    inputs, they'll tend to have more similar judgments.
*   When preference in different domains can be exchanged
    (e.g., doing someone a ritual favor in exchange for a
    special craft object), then preferences between clans
    across different domains can become more similar.

And although things are always situational, it does seem that
the big two factors will generally apply:

*   Alignment: Does helping clan A generally help us or hurt us?
*   Power: How much is clan A able to help us or hurt us?
    *   This may be constrained by norms. For example, in an
        egalitarian society, a 'ritual officer' may expect
        that if they try to use that role to gain power over
        people in other domains, they'll be punished by the
        group.

Judgments can eventually get into status-like constructs:

*   (A key theme here seems to be the level of reflexivity.)
*   People may start to talk about different preferences
    and judgments using ordinary language, but starting to
    fall into certain patterns or habits.
*   Eventually those patterns can be established as scripts
    and terminology, allowing things like achievement-based
    societies.
*   Eventually there can be rules and defined roles, now
    "official" and understood to be how society does and
    should work, as in "chiefly" societies.
    *   This would seem to be "all the way there" in terms
        of having rules-based organized societies. This is
        typically considered to be not quite a state, but
        precisely what differs that's so critical seems to
        be mushy. But I can at least name specific further
        steps:
        *   Writing down the rules
        *   Allowing the rules to be changed intentionally
            as a standard feature of society
        *   Less kin-based society
        *   (States are sometimes distinguished in terms of
            having hard power or social stratification, but
            it seems chiefly societies, and probably other
            less reflexive forms as well, were capable of
            having high levels of those.)
    *   Note also that hunter-gatherers would appear to often
        have rules, including rules against self-aggrandizement,
        so these categories here should not be seen as mapping
        in any clear way to traditional society types, even
        though there is an apparent correspondence.

At the moment, the key need is to better model village splits,
and what to update in the models around all this. We have 3 main 
status-touching areas to think about:

*   Marriage
    *   Look at existing model and make sure that:
        *   Clans match up via some sensible sort of preferences
            *   Align those preferences with discussion above
        *   Clan A's desire to marry clan B is influenced by how
            clan A thinks that will influence clan A's reputation
            and other relationships
    *   Start tracking long-term population growth and marriage
        success
*   Access to resources
    *   Eventually various things should be modeled in detail,
        especially access to land and ritual resources
        *   Land should be political process
        *   Need influence model
    *   For now, let's make this about key relationships:
            *   Farming and foraging help and favor exchange
            *   Care help and favor exchange
        *   There should be some differential in clans' ability
            to help each other and thus appeal as partners
*   Psychosocial stress
    *   If clans A and B neither want to defer to each other and
        they thickly react (e.g., demand sharing), high stress
    *   Clan A defers to clan B, lower stress for B, lower stress
        also for A if aligned enough

So for now, it seems clans will want to put themselves out there
as good partners. Based on later events in the area, it seems that
power centers also portrayed themselves like this at first, so,
looking ahead, perhaps ritual centers will try to be good partners
as mediators (powerful beings, and also humans), food banks, and
doctors.

But, we still need some sort of conflict process for people to
fall out over. Apparently, before new structures appeared, when
people didn't know each other well, there wasn't a bond of trust,
and they could easily fall out over mistakes. We can let influence
decide who comes off better, but that will apparently be mostly
via parnterships and support.
