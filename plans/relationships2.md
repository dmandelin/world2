The relationships model needs some help to come to life. Clans
need more decisions to make. Making a single attention value
primary makes it harder to understand the different kinds of
relationships. Highlighting a single interaction volume value
also mushes together benefits and makes it harder to understand
relationships as solving a set of problems in different ways.
Also, ideally conflict will be yet another type of interaction
in the same system, or an aspect of this system rather than an
unrelated item on the side. Relationships should cost something.

## Free writing exercise 1

We can start with the assumed background of hunter-gatherers.

One pattern is a reciprocal "friendship" and "gift exchange"
relationship that can be between unrelated people. These might
be more at the family than the band level, which we would model
as "small" relationships that can be cast somewhat widely. This
ostensibly primarly provides more food security and variety, but
it seems also to promote good relationships, information exchange,
and other kinds of help.

Some hunter-gatherers have specific ritual roles that link
people, such as who washed someone as a newborn. There's no
particular evidence these existed in our setting, but they
could have.

A big one is large gatherings. These can be very large and take
up quite a bit of time, amounting to temporary cities.

There are also specially trained healers and other such leaders
in some societies.

We can now think about what more permanent, denser settlements
would do to those existing relationships. Some of this might
convert to "neighbors" relationships that are kept up more cheaply
and have more frequent interactions. Large gatherings could be
held more cheaply and frequently. 

Apparently work feasts were a key new form of interaction with
farming.

Where does this leave us in terms of interactions? First, let's
think about the biggest things clans need from others:

*   Peace
*   Marriage partners
*   Help with:
    *   Food security - others may have food when we lack
    *   Medical problems - others may have special knowledge
    *   Spiritual issues - others may have special knowledge
*   Flint
*   Information:
    *   Entertainment
    *   Learning
    *   News

And in fact we already have clans with issues regarding marriage
partners, as one of the cities is out of range of the others. We
could allow certain relationships to extend the marriage range.

The part that's most highlighted right now is the help component,
so it would be good to try to fix that. The base here seems to
be some kind of reciprocal relationship. And it does make sense
for there to be an attention cost, but maybe also some additional
cost. Maybe we need some additional resource, perhaps called
"leisure", that does have benefits, so that anything that just
randomly takes work can use leisure. I suppose work itself should
actually use leisure, too, then. Maybe it's not just labor allocation
but also time allocation. There could also be travel involved, with
certain costs and hazards.

If clans pay the cost, then they can get some benefits, which
should at least include something about food security and good will,
but probably also marriage, trade, and information flowing too.

Next, we should clearly make the ritual model meaningful again.
The basic idea could be large gatherings which represent temporary
urban places and have those features. At these, people might
interact in a variety of ways depending on the scale. At the local
level, people might use a Communal Sharing model and get relatively
equal benefits. But at a larger level, there is probably some
Authority Ranking. These should also cost something: attention,
time, travel, but probably something above and beyond what's required
for inter-clan friendships: maybe special skills and resources,
or food to "waste".

Villages can presumably work somewhat like area gatherings,
but in their normal life they're not as intensive. They also
may cost less in terms of travel but maybe not infrastructure.
Villages would then want to have their festivals as temporary
more intensive interactions.

Villages can also add work feasts.

It would also seem that at the very beginning of our model there
probably are some sorts of "specialists". Ritual leaders and teachers
of farming, who might be the same people, are natural candidates.
Also doctors. We could keep them blended at first and then our ritual
skill model might still work. We might want to consider such people
being somewhat required to keep the farming economy growing, although
whether they're absolutely required is unclear.

Flint at first might be in small quantities and not work terribly
differently from some of these other components.

What's all this about information?

*   We should set it up so that learning skills from each other
    requires some sort of interaction, and in many cases specifically
    of working together.
*   Perhaps "Mood" or "Emotional regulation" is one of the needs, which
    can be addressed by entertainment, ritual, socializing, etc.
    We could imagine this as an appeal booster, but that's probably
    not right -- it's not true that people who have all their other
    needs met but not this one don't need any of it, though they 
    might need less
*   Interactions are of course required to exchange gossip on other
    clans.

And what about conflict?

For now let's think mainly about conflict in context of the relationships
we're describing.

*   For pairwise relationships, conflict can always arise within the
    relationship, over various aspects of how it should work. We might
    abstract this into the relationship for now but it could eventually
    become meaningful. There can always be a cooperate-defect dynamic
    here.
*   For exclusive relationships, there can be conflict over who the partner
    is.
*   The main kind of conflict we have so far is "field" conflict from
    people generally interacting in an area. We'll probably always need
    that, so to get symmetry we probably want to get some interactions
    on more of a "field" basis.

What do we want this to be able to do?

*   One need that's been out there is to get more interactions between
    villages, so anything here that adds to that is meaningful
    *   Also anything that implies things that scale
    *   And anything that implies special skills
    *   And anything that implies special roles
*   I really want more texture, more notion that clans can have a variety
    of relationships and connections with each other. 
    *   Specialty services and skills would help, since they naturally have

    *   Exclusivity also. Attention limits can add texture here.
    *   Finally, conflict is probably a major source of texture.

        *   Clearly there can be conflict over any sort of "premium"

            *   Ritual leadership positions (once implemented)
            *   Attention

Time vs Attention: Do we need both? Relationships probably should cost
work opportunities, so we'll need them to cost time. We should probably
also create the leisure concept, partly because it helps with certain
other problems. The questions are then, do relationships cost any other
nonmaterial resources, and if they do, do we care? In general, I think
they do. For example, there is the need to manage inevitable conflicts,
perform in various small ways, etc. But another way to look at this is,
if someone didn't need to work, could they do these relational activities
all the time? Clearly some people would not want to, but it seems in
theory, yes! So for now, in terms of the resource expended, it seems
we can work in terms of time

Mood and Happiness: It seems that emotional regulation is something that
as humans we need help with. The details are probably extremely complex,
but consider:

*   People in a clan of 50 won't get lonely or bored in the sense of
    having no one to talk to, but maybe things get stale, or maybe without
    any outside influences, certain clan members gain too much influence
    inside the clan, or there can be too much fighting.

*   It seems that people are usually not happy with very basic lifestyles
    where there's no novelty, challenge, or entertainment. Exactly why is
    unclear; some think maybe all that stuff is an accident. But another
    possibility is that repetition without variation is an unsafe state,
    and our instincts are tuned somewhat to require more.

The happiness model right now is additive, but that's probably not right.
How can we think about combining happiness better?

*   One simple idea is to take the min across the board. This reminds me
    of L_infinity magnitude, but that's a maximum. This is probably too
    strict.
*   Perhaps we can think of people as having needs, where they're unsatisfied
    if the needs are not satisfied, and then also certain positive concepts.
*   Understanding needs as badness means mathematical norm concepts can
    be applied
*   Then beyond that, all goods could maybe create some logarithmic goodness.
*   It's interesting that in income models, utility is often assumed to be
    log, so we have just one value, but psychologically it's not clear goods
    and bads really are seen the same.
*   If we use some rule like bads counting 5x more than goods that might
    make existing bads mostly dominant in about the right way.
*   Our bads so far are:
    *   Hunger
    *   Flood losses
    *   Migration costs
    *   Conflict (or does that just cause bad mood etc)
    *   Insufficient land
    *   Bad mood
*   Our goods so far are:
    *   Food surplus - but exactly how/why? break down via weaning
        practices, feasting, etc.
    *   Cereals (beer)
    *   Neighbor interactions
    *   Good mood
*   There should also be happiness of "positive purposes" of doing various
    things that people want. It's not clear what that would be at the start.
    Hunter-gatherers sometimes had dream quests, but it seems that here
    things were probably more communal. Maybe ever grander gatherings and
    gathering places were an early interest.
