Now that conflict, mutual aid, and migration are working in
rough form, the question of how to understand what clans
really think of each other returns.

Initial assumptions:

*   There may (or may not) be large status and social power
    differences in these villages
*   Status and social power differences at first are:
    *   Not that visible
    *   Not that permanent
    *   Not that convertible (not very currency-like)
*   As a result of the above, there may (or may not) be any
    kind of legible status ranking

Idea: Clans could randomly have prominent individuals who
give them some current bonuses.


So status exists, but is relatively fluid. We'll want to
understand its basis, and then mechanisms by which it might
become less fluid.

Reviewing previous notes, if we think of the concept as
centering around how much one clan might want to associate
with or imitate another, we have:

*   Marriage appeal
*   Partnership appeal (friends, mutual aid)
*   Technology imitation
*   Lifestyle imitation (farming vs fishing, leisure)
*   Specialist services (pottery, medicine, ritual)

If we let clans interact and sync their judgments, we can
end up with a sort of consensus appeal.

Observation: At this point, we're talking about relationship
desirability, not relative power or formal precedence, so
"status" is probably the wrong word and "appeal" a better one
(or perhaps some other term) for the judgment here.

In general, marriage appeal should depend things that make
sense historically, and things that are beneficial in the
model:

*   Value of partnership
    *   Will want to firm up the value of marriage
        relationships in other parts of the model
*   Skills and traits of the clan
*   Gifts

Going back to the early sources of productivity differentiation,
we have:

*   Land availability/quality
*   Skill
*   Assistance from other clans
