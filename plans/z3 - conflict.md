It's clear that conflict will be the engine of village
fissioning, because the local ecology generally will be
able to support more than Dunbar's number at a given
site. That means a clear conflict model is needed to get
village fissioning working.

# Initial discussion

The base concept is that interactions between people create
both amenities (marriages, help, news, etc) and disamenities
(conflict over family, resources, respect, priority, noise,
trash). In a very simple society, there aren't that many
different things to have or do together, so the amenities
come mainly from direct, friendly/trusted interaction (as
opposed to specialized products and infrastructure) and thus
scale with trust/communication boundaries. Disamenities can
come from any interaction. Thus, broadly, amenities will
scale up toward a limit, while disamenities will keep increasing,
although with diminishing (de)returns.

The model could be as simple as a flat disamenity score with
some material effect, perhaps some sort of consumption loss
and/or stress. However, having no differentiation by clan
doesn't seem a fit for the conceptual model built so far. So
I'll outline more detailed concepts and then assess how much
they help.

An abstract way to think about something more detailed would
be, what's the "production pipeline"? We might have:

*   Things to conflict over
*   Decisions over how to pursue the conflict
*   Results of the conflict
    *   "Material"
    *   "Psychosocial"
    *   Perceptions (happiness, respect, alignment)

Prototypical stakes/sources of conflict are:

*   Mates, marriages, and family
*   Land and other capital
*   Food and other resources
*   Damages (e.g., animals trampling crops)
*   Reputation
*   Arguments, interpersonal disputes, and sorcery

To understand these, we need to go further into "property",
sharing, status, and credit.

## Aboriginal Communal Sharing

Some societies have a norm of "demand sharing" in which if
A asks B for something, B is expected to give -- depending.
Perishable food windfalls must be shared, but apparently
items personally crafted at great labor may not be. However,
it's not as simple as a norm saying those don't have to be
shared. Rather, if A asks B for their finest coat, B may
depreciate the coat (thus reducing its ability to generate
prestige), saying that A wouldn't even want it, and give a
different gift instead. Or, the gift may work more as a loan,
e.g., they may ask for the coat back later. So, expensive 
durable property may be retained, but (a) has limited prestige
impact and (b) has an inventory cost of gifts, loans, and
favors required to retain the item within sharing norms.

This demand sharing system is associated with a high level
of psychosocial stress. It's hard for every A to track
how much B shared with C (e.g., so that A can know if B gave
A A's fair share), so there can be disagreements and conflicts
over this. People also try not to share in various ways.

In general (with demand sharing norms or otherwise), sharing
and gifts can be seen as a form of "social storage" (e.g.,
when can't store perishable goods) or "sale" for "social
credit". In Communal Sharing, presumably that's credit which
has to be maintained to a minimum value to retain group
membership, with more giving some slight rise in respect.
With Equality Matching, it's credit that has to be remembered
and properly reciprocated by the other party to be useful.
In Authority Ranking, this could be complicated but can be
perhaps understood as credit that buys into a tier and/or
outdoes rivals. 

Similarly, in some societies land is managed more or less
communally, e.g., a group of hunter-gatherers on a certain
range. There would generally be some sort of area rotations,
usage allowances, etc., to share out access. Thus, there
could be conflicts over over- or improper use.

## Storable delayed-return food

Storable delayed-return food creates significant changes.
Because it involves significant family labor, there is more
expectation of ownership. Communal sharing might still apply
to small donations, e.g., a family asking for one meal.

How exactly do we transition to ownership of harvests, though?
It's clear that if growers must demand-share their entire
crop, they won't have much incentive to grow. However, they
can depreciate their crop and share part of it. In some sense,
they pay a tax to everyone else in the village, and in exchange
their use right over the rest of the produce is validated.
They will have some status rise from their gifts, but mitigated.

A transition that can happen after that is when one clan gives
major help in the form of storable delayed-return food to
another. In that case, the debt might not be repayable, and
the debtor might owe "interest" in the form of service and
gifts indefinitely. Creditors can thus become "big men".

Other clans might want to shun a big man in order to keep
to egalitarian norms, but they might not want to. For one,
there could be bullying. But also, they're a relatively safe
"bank" for social credit.

In general, this process would seem to require unevenly
distributed surpluses. Without surplus, there aren't any
creditors. Without uneven distribution, no debtors. This also
suggests that the more surplus, the more credit-debt relationships.
This also seems related to Boltzmann distributions -- clans
can interact in a way that social capital (or other such)
gloms onto one.

Agricultural land is apparently managed as quasi-property. There
isn't necessarily clan ownership per se at first, but clans have
usage rights over land. If they're not using land, it will revert
to the village or to someone else. New land is doled out by a
political process, thus status and political skills are key.
Land can also be given in exchange for marriage partners. Finally,
it's possible to encroach on someone else's land allocation or
seize it through some political argument.

## Reputation and Status

Reputation can be considered multifactorial: different aspects
of information about a family would be salient for different
purposes. It can include:

*   Are they a proper member of the communal sharing group?
*   Do they pay their debts and keep their promises?
*   Do they engage in sorcery or otherwise harm others?

Clans will be highly motivated to keep their reputations. They
might also falsely attack the reputation of another clan, though exactly
why is less obvious. Arguments would certainly be one possibility,
or as a move in any other conflict.

Specific things clans will want/need status for:

*   Land ownership
    *   There is some persistence, but changes are largely negotiated
        through a political process.
    *   Secure rights require consent of neighbors.
*   Credit
    *   Other clans have a higher confidence that gifts will be
        reciprocated
*   Marriage
    *   More appealing to partners
*   Security and rights
    *   Key for less formal dispute resolution processes
    *   Also more psychosocial stress if lower status

## Hunting and Gathering

### Conflicts

What have we found sources of conflict for hunter-gatherers?

*   Land and resource use, especially under scarcity
    *   Indirectly depends on scale
*   Demand sharing arguments
*   Mating and marriage
*   Norm violations
    *   Refusal of sharing requirements
    *   Asserting authority over others
    *   Defying group consensus
*   Reputation and Status
*   Arguments and disputes

This is clearly way too complicated to add now, but perhaps the
most salient points are:

*   There are communal sharing relationships to be maintained
    that are hard to scale up
*   Mating rivalry can be a significant source of splits
*   Otherwise, resource conflict under scarcity is probably one
    of the biggest reasons for elevated conflict -- in our start
    state there aren't a ton of these resources so that should
    be able to kick in
*   Severe conflicts are often handled relatively simply by
    moving away

### Status

Key effects of status:

*   Membership
*   Marriage appeal
*   Credit
*   Stress

## Early Farming (with Foraging) Conflicts

*   Land use continues to be an issue but more permanence and
    investment will start to make this more important
    *   Farm land appropriation options
        *   This still has some sharing qualities but is more
            property-like
    *   Foraging land overuse options
*   Property damage such as animals damaging crops, or theft
    of crops, can now come up.

Here, resource scarcity should be less of a cause of fission,
but conflict over prime farmland could be. Demand sharing
arrangements still exist for foraging, and could continue
to cause conflict that way.

It would seem that none of our causes of conflict is necessarily
"the big one" for causing fissioning. Rather, there can be various
source of irritation, and many of them scale up superlinearly due
to being related to number of interactions, and the mechanisms
for dealing with them don't scale up that way. In fact, if it's
a consensus process, then we have limited bandwidth and/or will
start failing to synchronize at some point.

# Discussion for modeling

*   Land allocation models
    *   This can clearly be a source of conflict, and we already
        have these models
    *   Update these models to allow conflict mechanics, e.g.,
        land appropriation
    *   "Property tax" paid to the community
    *   Will need some sort of political resolution model
*   Consumable allocation models
    *   Scalar stress for demand sharing models
        *   These models probably should produce some level of
            redistribution, but not total, and might be somewhat
            mild for farming clans
    *   Rethink food sharing in terms of debt and credit
        *   Need to figure out appropriate forms of debt and
            credit per society and norms
*   Marriage
    *   Would be a good idea to make sure the model is matching
        in the right way and visualize it. See where to go from
        there
*   Reputation and Status
    *   Valid accusations of bad behavior
    *   Mistaken accusations of bad behavior
    *   Hostile defaming to try to gain status - but presumably
        risky
*   "General irritation"
    *   Probably the simplest thing and we do need it; scalar
        stress for demand sharing could perhaps be subsumed.
    *   Can have the costs be stress and consumables
    *   Scale effect by alignment so that people produce low
        irritation for friends but higher otherwise
